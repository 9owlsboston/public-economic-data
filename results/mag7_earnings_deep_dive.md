## Executive Summary

The Magnificent 7 collectively generated **$2,329,376M** in annual revenue, spent **$384,792M** on capital expenditures (16.5% of revenue), and invested **$210,405M** in R&D. Together they produced **$608,001M** in net income and **$400,344M** in free cash flow.

Three distinct archetypes are emerging:

1. **The AI Hyperscaler Bet** — **Meta**, **Alphabet**, and **Amazon** are pouring unprecedented capital into AI infrastructure. Combined capex for these three: **$292,957M** annually. The market is rewarding revenue growth but watching FCF margins closely.
2. **The Efficient Compounder** — **Microsoft** and **Apple** deliver consistent double-digit returns with disciplined capital allocation. Microsoft's 48% OCF margin and Apple's asset-light model (3% capex intensity) represent the "grow profitably" playbook.
3. **The Outlier Engines** — **NVIDIA** dominates every profitability metric (56% net margin, 45% FCF margin, 58% ROA) by selling the picks and shovels. **Tesla** is the cautionary contrast — negative revenue growth and 4% net margin make it the financial outlier in this group.

### The AI Capex Arms Race

The four hyperscalers (Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta) spent a combined **$357,508M** on capex in their latest fiscal year. To put that in perspective:

- That's **15.3%** of total Mag 7 revenue devoted to infrastructure
- It exceeds the **entire annual revenue** of all but ~30 companies in the S&P 500
- **Amazon**'s $131,819M alone is larger than **Tesla**'s total revenue ($94,827M)

Yet the market treats this spend very differently by company. **NVIDIA** and **Apple** prove you don't need massive capex to dominate — they achieve higher FCF margins (44.8% and 23.7%) by letting others build the infrastructure while capturing value through silicon design and platform control.

****Amazon**'s FCF squeeze deserves attention.** At 1.1% FCF margin on $716,924M in revenue, the company generates less free cash than **Tesla** despite being 7.5x larger. The $200B+ AI capex outlook cited in the AE note would push FCF negative unless AWS margin expansion accelerates.

## 11. So What — Strategic Implications

### For Microsoft Account Teams

1. **Microsoft is winning the "grow profitably" narrative.** Among the four hyperscalers, Microsoft has the best combination of growth (18.3% quarterly) and margins (38.3% net margin). Use this in customer conversations: "Our AI investments are accretive, not dilutive."
2. **Azure's growth outpaces the segment.** Intelligent Cloud grew ~30% but Azure grew 40% — meaning Azure is taking share within Microsoft's own portfolio. Server products (on-prem) are the drag. This acceleration narrative is powerful for cloud migration conversations.
3. **Capex discipline matters.** Microsoft's 37.3% quarterly capex intensity is high but below Meta (94.3%) and Alphabet (62.1%). The "below feared levels" capex from the AE note signals operational discipline that customers want from their cloud provider.

### For the Competitive Landscape

1. **Google Cloud's 63% growth is real.** When the Q1 2026 10-Q lands on EDGAR, verify whether this translates to segment profitability — Google Cloud only turned profitable in 2023 and margin trajectory matters more than top-line growth.
2. **Amazon's FCF problem is Microsoft's opportunity.** AWS customers may face price increases or reduced free-tier benefits as Amazon chases FCF expansion. Position Azure as the stable, profitable alternative.
3. **Meta's capex is a demand signal for NVIDIA, not a competitive threat.** Meta doesn't sell cloud — it buys GPUs. Every dollar of Meta capex is a dollar flowing to NVIDIA (and indirectly validating Azure's GPU capacity investments).
4. **Tesla is not a cloud competitor** but its financial stress makes it a potential cost-optimization customer. Companies under margin pressure often look to cloud migration to reduce on-prem infrastructure costs.
